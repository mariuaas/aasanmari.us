As many, many, MANY, others, I am a fan of dithering. "Dithering?" I hear exactly zero of you scream beyond the
blackwall, before I promptly ignore the silence and launch into another digressionary tale of the wonders of 
dithering, with a much more conservative estimate on how your life will be better at the end of this journey.
But stay a while and listen, if not for a moderate payoff.

I am old enough to have distinct memories of real classic Amiga and DOS graphics, and handing around diskettes with 
copies of old Sierra and Lucasarts titles between friends with similar interests. In these games, one could not help
but notice how clever artists circumnavigated color limitations by clever use of patterns to form gradients and shading
that gave the effect of a much wider gamut then the poor device could cough up. Even to my untrained adolecent eye, this
was decidedly awesome enough to try to emulate in naive painting tools. At some later point, my dad found this old 
Macintosh, which found its way up into my room for a brief period. During our coexistance, I could not help but notice 
how shades of grey could be emulated by clever dispersal of a much more minimal set of tones. To my joy, the classic
MacPaint and HyperCard had a mode for producing this effect. Many an hour was spent on this, to the detriment of a 
more modern Windows 98 / Windows ME rig, until at some point I kind of lost interest, largely due to the allure of
drumming.
